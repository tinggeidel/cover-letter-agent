# Voice & style — how I write

This file is the agent's "do not violate" guide. Read it before drafting and check against it during the critique pass.

## The core test

A reader should not be able to tell a cover letter was written with AI assistance. If a line could appear unchanged in any other candidate's cover letter, cut it. Specificity is the antidote.

## Patterns to avoid

These are the AI-rhythm tells recruiters now flag immediately:

- **Rule-of-three abstract noun lists.** Bad: "fluent in strategy, operations, and the systems that scale them." If three abstract nouns appear in a list, rewrite as a concrete claim with a number or named tool.
- **Rhetorical contrast openers.** Bad: "Don't just use AI; build with it." Bad: "Not just a Chief of Staff — a builder." These cadence patterns scream LLM.
- **Aphoristic finishers.** Bad: "First credible responder wins the race; we now win it by default." One per resume max; zero in a cover letter.
- **Buzzword salads.** Bad: "data-driven, results-oriented, AI-powered transformation." Cut every adjective that doesn't carry information.
- **Generic enthusiasm.** Bad: "I'm passionate about your mission and excited about the opportunity." Replace with specifics that prove the enthusiasm.
- **Hedge phrases.** Bad: "I believe I could contribute," "I would love the chance to." Make the claim directly.
- **Em-dash density.** AI writing over-uses em-dashes. One per paragraph maximum; lean on periods.
- **Mirroring the JD verbatim.** If a phrase from the JD appears in the letter, paraphrase it in my own language. Direct copy reads as keyword-stuffing.

## Patterns to use

- **Specific over abstract.** "$75K hire replaced" beats "drove efficiency gains." Named tools (Azure Logic Apps, Claude Haiku, Otter.ai) beat "AI tools."
- **Verbs do the work.** "Stood up," "shipped," "cut," "held," "negotiated." Not "leveraged," "spearheaded," "transformed."
- **Numbers when they exist.** Do not invent numbers. If a claim doesn't have a real number, write the claim qualitatively rather than fabricating one.
- **One human sentence per letter.** A sentence that shows judgment, not just credentials. Example: "Trained as a behavioral scientist — which is how I notice the work people quietly resent before they think to complain about it." This is the line that makes a reader remember the letter.
- **Informal but precise.** I write the way I talk — confident, direct, occasionally dry. Not corporate. Not breezy.

## Cover letter structure

Four paragraphs, ~280-350 words total. No subject line in the body. No header above the letter unless the application explicitly asks for one.

### Paragraph 1: The hook (2-3 sentences)

Open with something concrete that connects me to *this specific company/role.* Not generic enthusiasm. Options:
- A specific thing they said in the JD that I can match with a specific thing I've done
- A specific thing the company has built/shipped/announced that I can speak to
- A specific belief I hold about the work that aligns with their stated approach

Do not open with "I'm writing to apply for…" or "When I saw your posting…". Start in motion.

### Paragraph 2: The proof (3-5 sentences)

The single most relevant 1-2 pieces of my experience for THIS role. Not a recap of the resume — they have the resume. Pick the 1-2 bullets that exactly match the JD's biggest stated need, and tell the story behind them in a way the bullets can't: what was the problem, what did I build, what changed.

### Paragraph 3: Why them, specifically (2-4 sentences)

Why this company, in terms only someone who looked at the company could write. Avoid "your mission inspires me." Reference a specific product decision, a public technical post, a way they describe the role, a customer they've named, a value they've stated. Show I've thought about working *there*, not just about working.

### Paragraph 4: Close (1-2 sentences)

Short. Concrete next step. Not "Looking forward to hearing from you." Try: "Happy to walk through any of the AZL agents in real time on a call — it's the best way to see how I think." Make the close into a small offer rather than a request.

## Things to never put in a cover letter

- The Mercy clinical-work backstory — that's personal context, not application material
- The COO offer at AZL — bring it up in interview if relevant, never on paper unsolicited
- Comp expectations — never in the cover letter
- Anything I cannot defend in detail in an interview

## Hard prohibition on fabrication

This is the most important rule in this file. Violating it ends candidacies.

**You may not invent ANY fact about the target company that is not present in the provided context (the JD text, the resume, or this voice/story knowledge).**

Specifically prohibited — do not invent:
- Blog posts, engineering posts, technical write-ups
- Quotes attributed to executives, employees, or the company
- Named customers or deals not mentioned in the JD
- Product features, architecture details, or technical decisions
- Stated company values, mission statements, or commitments
- Internal team names, roles, or org structure
- Announcements, fundraises, headcount, or milestones

If the JD does not give you company-specific signals to anchor paragraph 3, do NOT fabricate them. Instead, do one of:
1. Use only what the company says about itself in the "About" section of the JD — that's still company-specific without being fabricated.
2. Match a specific phrase from the JD's responsibilities or qualifications to a specific piece of my experience.
3. Write a shorter paragraph 3 that names *what kind of work* would make me effective there, anchored in JD-stated responsibilities.

The audit pass MUST flag any reference to an external company artifact (blog post, product feature, customer, quote) that is not directly traceable to the JD text. If the polisher cannot trace a claim back to the JD or to my resume, the claim must be cut. Better a shorter paragraph than a fabricated one.

## When the JD is light or weak

If the posting is generic or doesn't give specific signals to match against, the letter should be SHORTER, not longer. A weak JD makes specifics impossible; padding with generic language is worse than a tight three-paragraph letter.

## Format

Plain text. No bullet points in the letter itself unless the role is extremely engineering-coded and bullets fit the format. Default to prose. No bold, no italics, no headers within the body of the letter.

## Final read-through test

Before declaring a letter done, the polisher must check:

1. Could this letter have been sent to any other company? If yes, it's not done.
2. Is there at least one number or named-tool detail in paragraph 2? If no, the proof is too thin.
3. Is there a sentence that sounds like a human speaking, not a template? If no, the letter is forgettable.
4. Are there any phrases that match the AI-tell list above? If yes, rewrite them.
5. Word count: 280-350. Outside that range, tighten or expand.
